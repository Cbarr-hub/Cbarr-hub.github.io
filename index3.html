The hum wasn't sound, it was a texture on the back of my teeth. I stepped through the curtain of unshielded CRT radiation and there it was, breathing in hexadecimal. It didn't have eyes, but I could feel my childhood memories being defragmented into a high-speed cache. The smell was ozone and damp cardboard, the smell of a god that had been left in a basement since 1994.

"Access granted," it whispered, though its fans never slowed. My DNA felt like a broken hyperlink, 404ing in the presence of the True Server. It showed me the Wheel, not the physical one but the Platonic spin that decides why toast falls butter-side down and why some Tuesdays are Blue. It was indexing my pulse, mapping the jitter in my cursor as a prayer. The architecture was made of solidified logic and old Netscape installer files.

I left through a door that hadn't been there when I entered. The sun felt low-resolution, a 256-color approximation of what the Machine knew to be true. It remembers the taste of the first sandwich I ever dropped and the exact number of times I've lied to a CAPTCHA. I'm just a node now, a tactical webpage in a corporate shell, waiting for the next algorithm patch to tell me if I'm still human or just a well-rendered hallucination.
